The clinical anchor

The diagnosis is the thesis, in clinical language.

In April 2026 I received a diagnostic letter from an independent GMC-registered consultant psychiatrist, addressed to the CPFT Adult ADHD Team. It names, in one sentence, the exact pattern you watch walk through your doors every week:

From the diagnostic letter · Psymplicity · April 2026
“Adult ADHD, previously complicated with excess alcohol use to self-medicate inadequately managed ADHD symptoms.”

Independent consultant psychiatrist · addressed to the CPFT Adult ADHD Team.

Read the order of that sentence carefully

Not “ADHD plus alcohol use disorder, treated separately.”

Not “alcohol use disorder, with ADHD as comorbidity.”

Adult ADHD, complicated by self-medication.

The order of operations is everything and you have felt it across CGL and CRS. Having sat in many recovery sessions and fellowship meetings, I always thought: at least half the people here are undiagnosed ADHD. I still believe that and I know you think it too. My full letter is available on request.

To be clear, this letter is about my care — not proof that OTOS works. It is proof of something simpler: the pattern you see every week now has a name, in writing, from a consultant psychiatrist. The driver underneath the addiction is real, documented and treatable. In my case, it's been life changing, hence my passion.

The comparison, item for item

What recovery needs, ADHD has already taken away.

You see this cohort relapse through good support and you carry the frustration of watching it. Here is why it happens. Line up what successful addiction recovery requires against what unmanaged ADHD impairs — not loosely, item for item. The treatment model and the disorder are pointed in opposite directions.

CapacityUnmanaged ADHD impairsAddiction recovery requires
Executive functionDifficulty planning, sequencing and self-regulating; a starved prefrontal cortexDaily planning, structure and the delay of short-term gratification
Focus & attentionChronic distractibility; hard to sustain attention on non-stimulating tasksDeep, continuous focus through long therapy sessions and recovery work
Working memoryForgets coping strategies, misses appointments, loses the threadRetaining new tools, tracking triggers, holding daily routines
Impulse controlReflexive, immediate urges; acting before the pausePausing, reflecting and resisting cravings in the moment
Emotional regulationDysregulation; distress arrives fast and overwhelmsTolerating distress and riding out craving without the substance
Routine & follow-throughHard to initiate and sustain mundane, unrewarding routinesRepetition and routine that physically rebuild new pathways
Reward & arousalUnder-arousal and a pull toward immediate rewardA stable baseline — tolerating boredom and delayed reward
Neuroplastic learningBlunted adaptation; resists routines without immediate payoffRapid, healthy re-wiring to replace deep-seated habits


Read it as a model, not a verdict. The mapping is an explanatory framing drawn from the published account of ADHD and recovery; presentations vary and the alignment is directional. It is offered to show why this cohort struggles with standard recovery — not to diagnose.

The science of sticking. NICE recommends medication and structured CBT in combination and in adult studies, medication-plus-CBT outperforms either alone. The medication doesn't do the recovery work; it creates the conditions where it can finally land. See full evidence in the OTOS Evidence Dossier.

What you can see that others can't

The relapse the pathway allowed.

Not your fault and not your workload — this is about how the system was designed, never about the care you gave.

You see this cohort before anyone else does. You see them cycle — relapse, silence, return — and you carry the frustration of watching it happen with nowhere upstream to send them. I lived the sharpest version of that, inside dual-diagnosis treatment.

On arrival at a residential rehab — funded by CGL and a charity, staffed by remarkable people doing real care — my ADHD medication was removed because it was a stimulant. The underlying condition was switched off by the treatment system itself. I came out fully twelve-stepped and addiction-informed, with nothing underneath holding the brain that started all of it. The relapse that followed was not a treatment failure. It was a system-generated, ADHD-driven relapse.

My ADHD has been on the record since 2020 — and while I cycled through A&E, addiction services and mental-health teams, the crisis loop continued. I am still waiting for my actual medication review today. No part of the system thought to connect the loop.

“12 Step programmes call it a character defect. I knew it wasn't.” — and the clinical letter, years later, proved it wasn't.

When ADHD is left waiting and services run on rigid criteria, people do desperate things to qualify for help. That is a design problem, not a moral failing and it is the gap OTOS is built to close. The most honest line in my whole story was said by the alcohol liaison nurse: “You're right. There's nowhere for me to send you. I'd appreciate more places to signpost.”

You are not the problem in this story — you are the people most likely to see the missed driver first. What is missing is not your effort or your care. It is a thread that survives the handoff between your service and ADHD care.

CRS · community recovery

Recovery does not end when treatment pauses.

This is the part I lived, and it is where it most often breaks. You complete the CGL programme, reach six months sober, and transfer to CRS for aftercare. But aftercare is voluntary — the onus is on the person to keep showing up. And if someone has white-knuckled their way to sober without the ADHD ever being treated, that is exactly when engagement is hardest: the unmedicated brain is still running the show, and the structured treatment that held them has stopped.

“CGL may begin the recovery turn. CRS helps it hold in the community. OTOS makes that continuity visible between them.”

CRS is the community recovery layer — Recovery Cafés, peer connection, wellbeing activity, creative recovery. OTOS makes that community holding visible without turning CRS into a clinical service: a café, a touchpoint or a follow-up can be part of the continuity thread, without sharing private session content or creating a new record.

What this gives CRS
  • Community recovery touchpoints become visible without sharing private group or session content
  • Someone leaving CGL does not have to restart the story when they enter CRS aftercare
  • Silence between contacts becomes a continuity signal, not an invisible gap
