The clinical anchor

The diagnosis is the thesis, in clinical language.

In April 2026 I received a diagnostic letter from an independent GMC-registered consultant psychiatrist — addressed to you, the CPFT Adult ADHD Team. It is medical correspondence about my care, not an endorsement of anything I am building. But it names, in one sentence, the exact pattern your assessments are designed to catch:

From the diagnostic letter · Psymplicity · April 2026
“Adult ADHD, previously complicated with excess alcohol use to self-medicate inadequately managed ADHD symptoms.”

Independent consultant psychiatrist · addressed to the CPFT Adult ADHD Team.

Please note the order of that sentence carefully.

Not “ADHD plus alcohol use disorder, treated separately.”

Not “alcohol use disorder, with ADHD as comorbidity.”

Adult ADHD, complicated by self-medication.

“Significant and comorbid substance misuse should be managed jointly between general adult and addiction services, with both teams aware of treatment plans and able to support each other.” Royal College of Psychiatrists — CR235, ADHD in Adults: Good practice guidance, 2023

The order of operations is everything. This is precisely the cohort your team exists to identify — and the cohort that, caught early, has the most to gain from your assessment. My full letter is available on request, and accompanies this brief by email.

This letter is about my care — not proof that OTOS works. It is proof of something simpler, and something only your team can act on: the driver underneath this kind of addiction is real, documented, and — once assessed and treated — genuinely treatable.

The comparison, item for item

What recovery needs, ADHD has already taken away.

You see this cohort relapse through good support and you carry the frustration of watching it. Here is why it happens. Line up what successful addiction recovery requires against what unmanaged ADHD impairs — not loosely, item for item. The treatment model and the disorder are pointed in opposite directions.

CapacityUnmanaged ADHD impairsAddiction recovery requires
Executive functionDifficulty planning, sequencing and self-regulating; a starved prefrontal cortexDaily planning, structure and the delay of short-term gratification
Focus & attentionChronic distractibility; hard to sustain attention on non-stimulating tasksDeep, continuous focus through long therapy sessions and recovery work
Working memoryForgets coping strategies, misses appointments, loses the threadRetaining new tools, tracking triggers, holding daily routines
Impulse controlReflexive, immediate urges; acting before the pausePausing, reflecting and resisting cravings in the moment
Emotional regulationDysregulation; distress arrives fast and overwhelmsTolerating distress and riding out craving without the substance
Routine & follow-throughHard to initiate and sustain mundane, unrewarding routinesRepetition and routine that physically rebuild new pathways
Reward & arousalUnder-arousal and a pull toward immediate rewardA stable baseline — tolerating boredom and delayed reward
Neuroplastic learningBlunted adaptation; resists routines without immediate payoffRapid, healthy re-wiring to replace deep-seated habits

Read it as a model, not a verdict. The mapping is an explanatory framing drawn from the published account of ADHD and recovery; presentations vary and the alignment is directional. It is offered to show why this cohort struggles with standard recovery — not to diagnose any individual.

The science of sticking. NICE recommends medication and structured CBT in combination — and in adult studies, medication-plus-CBT outperforms either alone. The medication doesn't do the recovery work; it creates the conditions where it can finally land. Full evidence in the OTOS Evidence Dossier.

Why the wait is the problem

On a list since 2020. A relapse the system built itself.

This is not a criticism of your team — it is the clearest case for why catching ADHD earlier matters so much.

My ADHD has been on a review list since 2020 — while I cycled through A&E, addiction services and mental-health teams, the crisis loop continued. I am still waiting for my medication review today. I lived the sharpest version of what that wait costs, inside dual-diagnosis treatment.

On arrival at a residential rehab — staffed by remarkable people doing real care — my ADHD medication was removed because it was a stimulant. The underlying condition was switched off by the treatment system itself. The relapse that followed was not a treatment failure. It was a system-generated, ADHD-driven relapse — the kind your assessment, made early, is built to prevent.

No part of the system thought to connect the loop — exactly the seam OTOS is designed to hold, with your team's assessment at the centre of it.

“12 Step programmes call it a character defect. I knew it wasn't.”

When ADHD is left waiting and services run on rigid criteria, people do desperate things to qualify for help. That is a design problem, not a moral failing and it is the gap OTOS is built to close. The most honest line in my whole story was said by the alcohol liaison nurse: “You're right. There's nowhere for me to send you. I'd appreciate more places to signpost.”

You are the answer in this story — the team best placed to catch the missed driver and switch the trajectory. What is missing is an agreed early route in, and a thread that survives the handoff back out.

How the thread works — and where you come in.

OTOS Continuity is a non-clinical layer that holds the thread between services. It does not diagnose, prescribe, treat or replace anyone. It surfaces signals; your team makes every decision. Nothing changes in how you work — what changes is what becomes visible.

What I'd love to see happen

Be the team that catches it first.

Everything here comes down to one small next step — and one genuinely exciting possibility: that the CPFT Adult ADHD Team may be among the first — and could be the first publicly documented NHS adult ADHD team — to establish a formal pathway with local addiction services aimed at identifying and treating ADHD as an upstream driver of addiction. It costs the system nothing it isn't already spending, and asks nothing outside what NICE already recommends. I would love to explore it with you.

What I'd love to see

I'm not asking you to approve OTOS, endorse NIA, commit to a pilot or take on new responsibility. A 30-minute conversation — no commitment beyond that. I'd value your clinical eyes on who would safely sit in and out of a small, jointly-agreed group of 50, and whether a clinically governed priority-review route, subject to CPFT criteria, partner agreement and governance, could let us test this together. On the public record, no NHS adult ADHD team has yet published a model like this — a chance to help write how it's done.

Corroborated by RCPsych CoSUM (“minimal collaborative working”) and the ADHD Taskforce, which names unsupported ADHD as a major route into substance misuse.
