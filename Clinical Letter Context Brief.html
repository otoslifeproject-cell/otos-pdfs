The clinical anchor

The diagnosis is the thesis in clinical language.

The clinical letter is medical correspondence about me — not an endorsement of anything I am building. It matters because it describes the ADHD-and-alcohol mechanism clearly, and because it places the right clinical question in front of the right pathway.

In April 2026 I received a diagnostic letter from Dr Fagbemi, consultant psychiatrist at Psymplicity, addressed to the CPFT Adult ADHD Team. It contains the most precise single-sentence statement of my situation I have ever read:

From the diagnostic letter · Psymplicity · April 2026
“Adult ADHD, previously complicated with excess alcohol use to self-medicate inadequately managed ADHD symptoms.”

Dr Fagbemi, Consultant Psychiatrist · addressed to the CPFT Adult ADHD Team.

Read the order of that sentence carefully.

Not “ADHD plus alcohol use disorder, treated separately.”

Not “alcohol use disorder, with ADHD as comorbidity.”

Adult ADHD, complicated by self-medication.

The order of operations matters, because it determines what the right treatment route looks like. Treating the alcohol alone left the driver underneath untouched. Recognising and treating the ADHD is what finally let the rest of the work hold.

This letter is about my care. It is not proof that anything I am building works. It is evidence that my pathway contains a clearly described ADHD / alcohol / self-medication mechanism worth understanding.

Not blame · Pathway learning

The system tried to help. Nothing held the space between services.

Addiction support, ADHD care, A&E, private treatment, recovery, referral, waiting and medication review all existed. They existed as fragments. The thread between them kept breaking.

On the list — not yet heldThe wait between services.
Illustrative · OTOS mock-up

A person can be clinically known and still operationally lost. I was known to many services and still fell through the space between them — not because anyone failed to care, but because no single service was resourced to hold the whole journey end to end.

Waiting does not hold this cohort safely. This cohort often does not disengage loudly — it drifts quietly between services, and by the time risk becomes visible again, it is frequently visible as crisis.

In my case that included repeated A&E presentations, detoxes, and a suicide attempt. I share this not for sympathy, and not to blame any one team, but because it is the human and system cost of a pathway no single service was funded to hold. It is also, plainly, a men's mental-health story.

I understand the pressure is real, and I am not pointing a finger at the ADHD Team or at CPFT. The gap I am describing is structural — it sits between services, which is exactly where no single team is funded to stand.

Already in motion

This conversation is already starting on the addiction side.

I am not bringing you a cold idea. The people who held me through the worst of this — keyworkers and team leaders in addiction and recovery services — already recognise the pattern, because they see it every week. In their own words, several are keen to help open this conversation, and some are, quite literally, one email away from contributing.

  • Change Grow Live (CGL) and Cambridgeshire Recovery Service (CRS) are central, because the cohort is already visible to them — adults cycling through relapse, silence and return, where unrecognised or under-treated ADHD may be the missed driver.
  • This is not asking keyworkers to become ADHD clinicians. It is asking whether a small, jointly-agreed group might be carrying the same driver I carried.
  • The goodwill already exists. What is missing is a shared continuity layer to hold it together, and the ADHD Team's clinical judgement to shape it safely.
And there is a clear precedent on the addiction side. Change Grow Live has already published practice-development work — through Nottingham CVS's Practice Development Unit — on psychotherapeutic and diagnostic assessment approaches for ADHD within substance-use services, mapping the real barriers in this cohort: symptom overlap, substance-induced symptoms, stigma, missed appointments, chaotic lifestyle. ADHD is already being recognised inside the addiction context. OTOS does not duplicate that clinical work — it goes one step further, making continuity visible between the addiction and ADHD pathways so the cohort, once identified, is fast-tracked and held rather than lost.

Source · CGL / Nottingham CVS Practice Development Unit — “Psychotherapeutic assessment approaches in the diagnostic assessment of ADHD.” My honest view: this conversation will happen with or without me — far better it happens carefully, locally, with the CPFT ADHD Team in the room from the start.

Could this lived pathway help shape something for others?

Separately from my care, I have been building OTOS Continuity — a non-clinical layer that helps hold the thread between services. It does not diagnose, prescribe, treat or replace anyone. It surfaces signals; partner teams make the decisions.

OTOS elementWhat it doesWhy it matters
Micro check-insSimple signals between appointmentsHelps show whether someone is still connected, or starting to drift
Transition receiptsRecords whether a handoff happenedReduces the risk of referrals disappearing into silence
Drift visibilityFlags missing follow-throughMakes risk visible earlier, for human review
Human review queueKeeps decisions with peopleAvoids any automated clinical decision-making
Tokenised continuity trailHolds minimal continuity signalsGives pathway visibility without becoming a new clinical record

Why the ADHD Team's involvement would matter.

  • You understand ADHD diagnosis, titration, medication review and the reality of shared care.
  • You can see what is clinically sensible — and what is not — and help define safe inclusion / exclusion criteria and what may be shared.
  • Your involvement could be the difference between a good idea and a safe, clinically credible pathway-learning model.

Explored safely and under qualified clinical leadership, Cambridge & Peterborough could help pioneer a careful, possibly first-of-its-kind local test of continuity learning for ADHD-driven addiction.
