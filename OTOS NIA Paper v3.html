The declaration

Name the pattern the system keeps missing.

This is not ordinary comorbidity.

Current frameworks treat ADHD and alcohol dependence as two separate problems that happen to overlap. This paper proposes something more specific: a pattern in which alcohol first does the neurological work that untreated ADHD leaves undone — and only later crosses into dependence. Neurologically Informed Addiction (NIA) is the proposed name for that pattern.

"I did not invent the suffering. I named the pattern."
NIA is proposed, founder-originated terminology — not an official diagnosis. It is offered for clinical consideration, research and service design, alongside and never replacing existing diagnoses and services. Everything in this paper sits inside that boundary.

The pattern, in one page

A single pathway, not two passing conditions.

A large share of adults with untreated or under-treated ADHD self-medicate. For many, the substance that works most reliably — because it is legal, accessible and socially normalised — is alcohol. In the proposed NIA pattern, alcohol does not begin as misuse. It begins as regulation — relief, focus, energy, social access — for a brain that, without language for ADHD, is reaching for something that finally steadies it.

Over years, that functional use can cross an invisible line into physical and psychological dependence. By the time help is sought, the system sees the dependence and treats it — while the original ADHD-shaped driver underneath is mis-sequenced in the pathway or missed entirely. Treating the alcohol alone leaves the driver untouched.

The core argument of this paper is simple and once seen, hard to un-see: addiction recovery demands precisely the cognitive capacities that unmanaged ADHD impairs — executive function, attention, working memory, impulse control, emotional regulation, routine and delayed reward. That is why standard recovery so often fails this group and why recognising and treating the ADHD will be what finally lets recovery work land for those found with it being the barrier to recovery.

Where dual-diagnosis falls short

Two silos cannot hold one pathway.

The prevailing model is "dual diagnosis": ADHD and a substance use disorder treated as distinct conditions that co-occur. That framing is not wrong but for this cohort it is incomplete. It describes co-occurrence not function or sequence and the gap between the two silos is exactly where people are lost. "Known" by services is not the same as "held".

Today · dual diagnosis
ADHD
known to both · held by neither
Alcohol

Two parallel tracks that never meet. Each side waits for the other — and the person drifts in the gap between them.

Proposed · NIA
one driven pathway

ADHD recognised as the driver, sequenced correctly, and the person held across services — clinically known and operationally held.

The Royal College of Psychiatrists already advises that significant, comorbid substance misuse "should be managed jointly between general adult and addiction services" (CR235). NIA proposes the category that would make that joint management routine for the ADHD-driven alcohol cohort — not exceptional.

The cohort, anchored — the receipt exists

That sentence is the cohort.

"Adult ADHD, previously complicated with excess alcohol use to self-medicate inadequately managed ADHD symptoms."

Dr Fagbemi, Consultant Psychiatrist, Psymplicity — diagnostic letter, April 2026 · personal details redacted.

That single clinical sentence is the whole thesis in miniature. Not ADHD and alcohol use as two separate problems — adult ADHD, complicated by self-medication. The letter is medical correspondence about one person. It is not an endorsement of OTOS, and not proof that OTOS works — but it documents, in clinical language, that the mechanism is real in a lived pathway.

Founder proof is not population proof. One documented case shows the pattern exists and that the pathway failed to name it. The proposed category, and the research around it, exist to test whether it holds for a defined cohort.

The founder's correction

Alcohol was not misuse at the start.

This is the correction that matters most, and the one most often flattened into generic "misuse" language. In the beginning, alcohol was not abuse. Alcohol worked. It gave focus, energy, relief, confidence, social access and a kind of regulation — before there was any language for ADHD, before any diagnosis, before anyone had named what the brain underneath was actually doing.

It was legally bought, easily accessible and socially normalised. It functioned, in effect, like unrecognised neurological regulation — the steadying that medication might have provided, had ADHD been recognised in time. To call that "misuse" at the outset misreads what was happening: it was self-medication of an unmet neurological need, using the most available tool there was.

"It did not start as a problem. It started as the only thing that worked. Needing a drink wasn't about curing hangovers or settling shakes"
This is not a defence of drinking and not a claim that alcohol is safe. It is a precise account of sequence: function first, dependence later. That sequence is what the proposed NIA pattern is built to recognise and what generic "alcohol misuse" language erases.

Dean ButlerThe invisible line · 06
06
Function becomes dependence

Somewhere, it crossed a line nobody saw.

The danger of a substance that genuinely works is that the line into dependence is invisible while you cross it. What began as regulation slowly became requirement. The brain adapts: the same amount does less, more is needed to feel level and stopping no longer returns things to baseline — it drops below it. By then the alcohol is no longer steadying the ADHD; it is its own problem, layered on top of the first.

the invisible line
Before the line

Function

Alcohol regulates an unrecognised ADHD brain — focus, relief, social access. It works, so it continues.

Crossing

Tolerance

The brain adapts. More is needed for the same effect; use becomes daily, then structural. The line is crossed unseen.

After the line

Dependence

Physical and psychological dependence. Now there are two problems — and the system usually only treats the second.

Crucially, around the period a person finally wants to stop and seeks help is often when ADHD is first recognised. But recognition is not sequencing: in the lived case, the alcohol dependence worsened while the ADHD stayed mis-ordered in the pathway — diagnosed but not yet treated in a way the brain could use.

The science of the pathway

Why the wrong chemistry makes recovery so hard.

The mechanism is, in plain terms, a chemistry mismatch. The unmanaged ADHD brain tends to run with lower available signalling in the systems that govern focus, motivation and self-control. A substance that temporarily lifts that signalling feels, at first, like relief but long-term dependence then erodes the very systems recovery depends on.

The chemistry

Dopamine & noradrenaline

These catecholamines regulate reward, motivation, arousal and attention. In ADHD, their signalling in key circuits is widely understood to run low — leaving a brain that is under-aroused and reaching for stimulation.

The control centre

The prefrontal cortex

The seat of executive function — planning, judgement, impulse control. It depends on steady catecholamine signalling to work. Under-fuel it and pause-and-reflect becomes reflex-and-react.

The trap

The reward hijack

A substance floods the reward system with a surge the ADHD brain rarely feels naturally. The brain learns the substance equals relief and, over time, turns down its own sensitivity to ordinary reward.

The cost

Neuroplastic learning

Recovery is re-wiring: building new habits through repetition. That needs a working prefrontal cortex and a stable baseline — the very things untreated ADHD and long-term dependence both degrade.


An explanatory model. Neurochemistry is simplified here for clarity. "Low dopamine" is a widely-used explanatory framing, not an absolute or settled measurement and individual presentations vary. The point is directional, not a claim of precise mechanism.

A loop that tightens the longer it runs.

Put the chemistry in motion and it becomes a self-tightening loop. Each turn makes the next one harder and makes standard, abstinence-first recovery less likely to hold, not more.

Untreated ADHD: low arousal, restless focus, impulsivity, emotional swings. The brain is hunting for something that steadies it.

02

Alcohol regulates

sets in

Long-term use worsens the underlying deficits, flattens neuroplasticity and erodes prefrontal control — deepening the original problem.

Now recovery asks for focus, memory and impulse control — the exact capacities most degraded. Failure looks like "lack of willpower." It is not.

Breaking the loop is not about trying harder. It is about restoring enough neurological stability — through proper ADHD treatment under clinical care — that the recovery tools become usable. Treat the driver and the addiction work can finally hold.

Look at the two lists side by side

This can't be coincidence.

Everything addiction recovery requires, unmanaged ADHD impairs. Not loosely. Item for item. Executive function. Attention. Working memory. Impulse control. Emotional regulation. Routine. Delayed reward. The capacity to learn new habits at all.

When the requirements of recovery and the deficits of ADHD line up that precisely, the question stops being whether they are related and becomes why we keep treating them as if they are not.

The pathway is the point

The medicine opens the door. Continuity holds it open.

If the driver is neurological, then stabilising the neurology — through proper ADHD treatment, under qualified clinical care — may be the missing prerequisite that lets recovery work finally land. But medication alone is not the intervention. Medication opens the door; Continuity holds it open long enough for the person, the service and the recovery work to participate properly.

Medicine-plus-Continuity means clinical ADHD stability wrapped in adherence support, relapse-risk visibility, service continuity, recovery support and behavioural reinforcement — so gains are not lost between appointments, services or motivation dips. The molecule is not the product. The pathway around it is.

This reframes the published evidence carefully. START is a comparator signal: after ADHD diagnosis and methylphenidate initiation, improvement in addictive disorders was observed in a study population. That does not prove OTOS, and it does not make medication a cure. It supports the narrower proposition that treating the ADHD driver may make addiction recovery work more usable for a defined subgroup.

Dean ButlerThe lived sequence · 12
12
Methylphenidate → lisdexamfetamine

The right medicine, seven years too late.

The founder's sequence matters because it shows that recognition is not the same as treatment. At diagnosis, methylphenidate was started, but the treatment was not properly continued after a GP change. The next seven years included six A&E presentations seeking detox, police and probation involvement, a suicide attempt, two intensive-care admissions, repeated CGL engagement, day-hab, residential dual-diagnosis rehab and repeated failed sobriety attempts. The rehab was caring and intensive — but on arrival his ADHD medication was removed because it was a stimulant. He left with addiction knowledge, therapy and structure, but with no medication underneath it. He remained on the NHS ADHD medication-review waiting list. A final relapse forced the question he had carried for years: was the untreated ADHD the driver? He engineered a private reassessment and was prescribed lisdexamfetamine, better matched to his combined-presentation ADHD. Only then did the same recovery work — courses, therapy, structure and repetition — become usable.

"The work was not wrong. For a while after rehab, it held. But without medication and without alcohol, I was living with pure ADHD for the first time in my adult life. Relapse was not a failure of effort. My brain was still seeking the basic function it did not have."
Said plainly, to be safe: this is one clinically supervised case, not a population study and not a claim that lisdexamfetamine — or any ADHD medication — cures alcohol dependence or is right for everyone. Medication choice, titration and supervision are clinical decisions. OTOS does not diagnose, prescribe, manage or promote medication. The point is the sequence: treat the driver properly, and recovery may finally hold.

Stated formally

NIA — the proposed definition.

Proposed category · founder-originated
Neurologically Informed Addiction (NIA)

A proposed subtype of addiction in which substance use — most commonly alcohol — begins as functional self-medication of an underlying neurodevelopmental condition, principally ADHD and later progresses to dependence. In NIA, the neurology is understood as a driver of the addiction, not merely a co-occurring condition and treatment of that driver, under qualified clinical care, is considered a prerequisite for recovery to hold, rather than a secondary concern.

The lived origin term

A "Double Trouble" Diagnosis

The raw, lived name for ADHD and addiction running as one loop. Used in the founder narrative only.

The proposed category

Neurologically Informed Addiction

The professional, clinical-facing term offered for consideration, research and service design.

NIA is proposed terminology, not an official diagnosis. It is offered for clinical review and research, alongside — never replacing — DSM-5 and ICD-11 categories and existing services.

What would define the cohort

The signals that would mark an NIA pathway.

Offered for discussion, not as a diagnostic instrument: the features that, taken together, might distinguish an NIA pathway from alcohol dependence alone. A clinician would confirm any of this — OTOS would not.

1

ADHD foundation

A confirmed or strongly indicated ADHD diagnosis underlying the alcohol pathway.

2

Alcohol dependence / harmful use

Established dependence or harmful use — most commonly alcohol — meeting recognised criteria.

3

Functional-regulation history

Early alcohol use that improved focus, calmed restlessness, steadied mood or created social/executive access.

4

Executive-relapse pattern

Relapse driven by executive exhaustion, boredom, task paralysis or under-arousal, not cues alone.

5

Treatment mismatch

Standard recovery repeatedly fails for reasons linked to forgetfulness, distraction, impulsivity or poor follow-through.

6

Response-to-sequence signal

Engagement improves once ADHD is properly recognised, treated or stabilised under qualified clinical care.

7

Continuity need

The person is known to services but not held across the whole pathway.

8

Repeated crisis contact

A&E, detox, ICU, crisis mental health, safeguarding, police or probation contact recurs without the thread connecting.

9

Mental-health engagement history

Therapy, antidepressants, anxiety/depression treatment or counselling has occurred without the ADHD/alcohol sequence being held.

10

Recovery-service recycling

Repeated onboarding into CGL, CRS, detox, day-hab, rehab, AA or recovery support without durable resolution.

11

Waiting-list vulnerability

ADHD assessment, titration or medication review is delayed while addiction risk remains active.

12

Cross-service evidence trail

Letters, discharge notes, referrals or partner observations reveal the same pattern across services, but no one system owns it.

Bold on pattern · honest on proof

What the evidence already points to.

None of these are OTOS outcomes. They are anchors and signals pointing in the same direction — strong enough to justify formal review and research, not strong enough to claim NIA as settled.

Verified / documented anchors
  • Clinical letter anchor — "self-medicate inadequately managed ADHD symptoms." One documented case, not population proof.
  • RCPsych CR243 — adult ADHD co-occurs with SUD: earlier onset, greater severity, reduced treatment effectiveness. Published guidance.
  • RCPsych CR235 — substance misuse may be ~2× as common in ADHD; manage jointly across services. Published guidance.
  • NICE NG87 — substance-misuse history is a context where ADHD prevalence may be raised. Published guidance.
  • NHS ADHD Taskforce — unsupported ADHD carries major whole-system cost and harm. Macro context.
  • Founder service trail — A&E, ICU, CGL, rehab, probation, psychiatry and ADHD waiting-list contact without the thread held. Lived pathway evidence.
Candidate / comparator signals
  • START 61.3% — improvement in addictive disorders after ADHD treatment in a study population. Comparator only; not an OTOS outcome.
  • ADHD prevalence in addiction cohorts — reported elevated (23–45%). Published / candidate depending on source pinning.
  • BMJ / Southampton — reduced substance-misuse events with ADHD medication. Candidate; citation pending.
  • Am. J. Psychiatry — reported reductions in substance-use crises. Candidate; citation pending.
  • 2–3× risk estimates — elevated SUD risk in ADHD by method/population. Candidate; CR235 ~2× is the safer anchor.
  • Local demonstrator need — a bounded local test to replace modelling with evidence. OTOS research rationale.
The position, in a line: the evidence is strong enough to justify recognition and research, and not yet strong enough to claim NIA as settled.

Category · intervention · infrastructure

Where OTOS sits — and what the layer actually does.

NIA is the category. Medicine-plus-Continuity is the intervention. OTOS Continuity is the infrastructure: a non-clinical engagement layer that keeps the person active, visible and learning between services. It does not just record handoffs. It delivers micro check-ins, pathway reminders, waiting-well learning, partner-branded messages where agreed, appointment prompts, recovery-literacy nudges, human-review queues and consent-led engagement signals. Those repetitions matter. They keep the new behavioural pathway warm while ADHD treatment, addiction recovery, therapy, community support and clinical services do their work. Continuity is not the clinical intervention. It is the layer that helps every existing intervention land more consistently.

Surface

Micro check-ins, drift visibility and relapse-risk signals before crisis is the only signal.

Hold

Appointment prompts, transition receipts, partner messages and human review so the person stays connected between services.

Reinforce

Waiting-well learning, ADHD/recovery literacy, CBT-style repetition and behavioural nudges that help new habits stick.

Evidence

Consent-led signals, engagement tracking and learning reports so claims can be tested, improved, proved or stopped.

The firewall is the design. OTOS does not diagnose ADHD, prescribe, manage or promote medication, treat addiction, deliver therapy, replace any service or make clinical decisions. OTOS surfaces signals; partner teams make the decisions.

And the process now under way

If you name it, you can hold it.

Naming a pathway changes what the system can do with it. A named NIA pathway could be screened for, sequenced correctly, commissioned, audited and researched — instead of being repeatedly rediscovered, one crisis at a time. Recognition is not vanity; it is what turns a lived insight into something a service can act on.

Out the other side

Surviving the journey, now the pattern can be named.

This paper is not a pitch and not a request for anything. It is a statement of position: what our Founder navigated, what the pattern is, why the science makes it credible, what the published evidence already shows and where the boundaries sit. NIA is offered for clinical consideration and research — to be tested, challenged and refined by people more qualified than its founder.

The position, in a sentence

A cohort the system already sees and already pays for is likely driven by untreated ADHD beneath the visible addiction — naming that pathway, treating the driver and holding the person across services could change what recovery is even able to do. That is worth recognising properly.
